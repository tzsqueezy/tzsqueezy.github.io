Grip finder — analytics & de-risking framework

Golf Pride grip finder · Product strategy · Q2 2026

What the quiz funnel data needs to answer

Where exactly do users abandon?

Not just overall completion rate — you need per-question drop-off. The question with the steepest cliff is the highest-priority rewrite or removal candidate.

GA4 / funnel events

How long does each question take to answer?

High time-on-question signals confusion, not consideration. The hand measurement step almost certainly shows the longest dwell — confirming this validates redesigning it as a visual guide.

GA4 / session recording

What percentage of completions lead to a product page click?

This is the core conversion gap. If completions are high but clicks are low, the result page is the problem. If completions are low, the quiz itself is the problem.

GA4 / goal funnels

What percentage of product clicks from quiz results become add-to-cart or purchase?

Isolates whether the quiz is generating qualified intent or just curiosity clicks. Quiz-assisted purchases vs. unassisted is the business case metric for leadership.

GA4 / attribution

Do mobile users complete at a lower rate than desktop?

If yes, this de-risks investing in mobile UX improvements first before any content or logic changes. Eliminates the question of whether it's the questions or the interface.

GA4 / device segmentation

Do users who enter from product pages complete at a different rate than those from nav?

If product-page entrants complete more (higher intent), it validates the inline distribution strategy. If not, it means the quiz needs to work harder regardless of entry point.

GA4 / traffic source

Site behavior signals that shape placement strategy

How do users who visit the quiz page arrive there?

If most come from direct nav rather than product pages, the current site isn't surfacing the quiz at moments of purchase intent — which directly validates the distribution strategy.

GA4 / previous page path

What do users do after viewing a grip product page without buying?

If they bounce or go back to the category — rather than going to the quiz — it means the bridge between "I'm looking at a grip" and "I need help choosing" doesn't exist yet. That's the core gap the inline module fills.

GA4 / next page path

How many product pages does the average user visit before purchasing?

High page count before purchase = high uncertainty = high value for a recommendation tool. If users browse 4+ grip pages before converting, the inline module has a very clear ROI story.

GA4 / pages per session

Which grip product pages have the highest bounce rate?

These are the pages where users are least able to self-qualify. They're the highest-priority placements for the inline 2-question module — biggest potential impact, clearest need.

GA4 / page-level bounce

What search terms bring users to the site or quiz?

If users arrive via "which golf grip is right for me" type queries, SEO-optimized quiz entry pages could be a low-cost acquisition layer. Also reveals the language users use vs. Golf Pride's copy.

Google Search Console

What is the quiz's contribution to total site sessions — and is it growing?

Establishes the current baseline reach. If it's under 5% of sessions, even a well-optimized quiz is low-reach without distribution changes. Makes the case for the nav entry point.

GA4 / page reach %

Qualitative data to collect and analyze

Session recordings on the quiz

Watch for hesitation, backtracking, rage clicks, and scroll behavior. Hotjar or FullStory. Most revealing on the hand measurement question.

Exit survey on the result page

1-question: "Did this recommendation feel right for you?" Yes / Mostly / Not really. Even 50 responses tells you whether the logic is trusted.

On-page poll at quiz entry

"What brought you here today?" — first-time buyer / replacing current grip / curious. Reveals intent mix without requiring a long survey.

Customer support / chat logs

What grip-related questions are asked most? Repetition = quiz isn't answering the question users actually have. Mine for copy angles too.

Post-purchase survey

"How did you choose your grip?" — reveals how many buyers used the quiz, ignored it, or didn't know it existed. Critical for reach baseline.

Moderated user testing (3–5 sessions)

Ask users to find a grip they'd buy. Watch where they go, what confuses them, whether they discover the quiz. Reveals navigation assumption failures.

Review and forum mining

Golf forums, Amazon grip reviews, Reddit. What language do golfers use to describe grip problems? Maps to quiz question framing.

Sales rep / retailer interviews

What questions do in-store customers ask before buying? These are the questions the quiz should replicate — validated by real purchase behavior.

The single most valuable qualitative question to ask

Ask 5–8 recent grip buyers: "Walk me through how you decided which grip to buy." The answer almost always reveals whether the quiz maps to their real decision journey — or whether it asks about things they never actually thought about.

What each data finding tells you to do

If you find...

Drop-off spikes on the hand measurement question

Prioritize the visual hand-measurement guide as the single highest-ROI design change. Don't redesign the full quiz first.

If you find...

High completion rate but low click-through from results

The quiz logic is fine — the result page is the problem. Invest in the "recommendation reveal" redesign and clearer CTAs before touching the questions.

If you find...

Low completion on mobile vs. desktop

Do mobile UX fixes before any content strategy work. A better-designed question won't help if the interface is the blocker.

If you find...

Quiz accounts for under 5% of site sessions

Reach is the problem, not quality. Reprioritize nav visibility and inline placement over quiz redesign — more people need to find it before you optimize it.

If you find...

Users browse 4+ product pages before buying

Strong ROI case for the inline module on product pages. Use this as the quantified business case in the roadmap meeting.

If you find...

Post-purchase survey: most buyers didn't use or find the quiz

Distribution is more urgent than redesign. Sequencing shifts — get the quiz visible before making it beautiful.

If you find...

Exit survey: "recommendation didn't feel right" scores are high

Trust in the output is the problem — points to question logic and result explanation copy as the core investment, not visual design.
