Grip finder — complete kickoff framework

Golf Pride grip finder · Project kickoff + continuous discovery · Q2 2026

Integrates Teresa Torres's Continuous Discovery Habits (CDH) throughout

The 6 goals of this kickoff

01

Frame outcomes, not outputs

Establish from the first minute that this meeting is about what measurably better looks like — not what to build. Ground rules are set here: solutions go in the parking lot until there's an opportunity to hang them from.

02

Align on a shared problem statement

Everyone leaves able to say the same sentence about what this project is solving and why now. A draft is prepared in advance for the room to react to — not written from scratch in the meeting.

03

Build and pressure-test the opportunity solution tree

A draft OST — outcome at the top, opportunity branches below, solutions column blank — is introduced for stakeholders to reshape. This structures debate around customer needs, not internal opinions. Disagreements about which branch matters most are the most valuable moments in the room.

04

Define measurable success outcomes

Not a vision statement — specific, measurable changes in customer behavior or business results that define a clear win. Agreed on by the group, mapped to the OST outcome node. 2–3 metrics maximum.

05

Establish the discovery rhythm

CDH principle: research is a continuous habit, not a one-time phase. The kickoff names who runs customer interviews, how often, and how learnings feed the OST. Without naming this owner and committing to a cadence in the room, the habit stops within two weeks.

06

Clarify roles, decision rights, and OST ownership

Who owns what? Who decides when there's no consensus? Who maintains the OST as the living project document? RACI drafted before the meeting, validated in the closing minutes. Two calendar dates booked before leaving the room.

What this kickoff is NOT for

Designing solutions. Debating feature priorities. Reviewing competitor screenshots. Pitching ideas. All of that comes after research — and only after an opportunity has been validated with enough customer evidence to justify it. The kickoff protects against skipping to solutions before understanding the problem.

How to use the OST in the room

Put the outcome and the three opportunity branches up on screen. Ask: "Are these the right opportunities? What's missing? Which branch do we believe is most critical to address first?" This discussion — not a solution debate — should anchor the first half of the meeting. A solution only earns the right to enter the tree once the opportunity it serves has been validated by customer research.

Pre-work — what to prepare before the kickoff and who owns it

PM / analyst

Analytics snapshot — 1 page maximum

Pull and distribute 48 hours before the meeting: quiz reach as % of total site sessions, per-question completion and drop-off rates, result-to-product-click rate, quiz-assisted purchase attribution, and mobile vs. desktop completion split. Sending this in advance means the meeting doesn't waste time orienting everyone to basic facts — and debates about what's actually happening can be short-circuited with data already in the room.

PM

Draft problem statement

Don't ask the room to write a problem statement from scratch — that's a 45-minute spiral. Send a draft in advance and ask people to come with reactions. Example: "The grip finder reaches too few users and converts too few of those who complete it — we don't yet know whether the core problem is reach, the quiz experience, or trust in the result." Much faster to edit than create.

PM

Draft OST — outcome + opportunity branches

The three-branch OST prepared as a one-page visual. Sent with the analytics brief so stakeholders arrive with a mental model of the opportunity space before the meeting. The solutions column stays blank — this signals that the session is about opportunities, not feature pitches.

PM

Project context one-pager

What we know, what we don't, what's in scope for this phase, and what's explicitly out of scope. Include a brief note on the CDH approach: this project will use continuous weekly customer interviews as the primary discovery method — research is not a phase with an end date.

UX lead

3–5 inspiration references (optional)

Not to anchor solutions, but to establish a shared aesthetic and UX quality bar. Framed as "this is the experience standard we're aiming for" — not "this is what we're building." Prevents vague words like "premium" or "elegant" from meaning different things to different people when the design phase begins.

Facilitator

RACI draft for research and design phases

Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed — drafted before the meeting, validated in the closing 10 minutes. Includes a named OST keeper: the person responsible for updating the tree after each interview and experiment. Ambiguity here is the single biggest cause of slow projects with multiple stakeholders.

All stakeholders

Pre-read prompt sent with invite

A single ask sent with the calendar invite: "Come with one assumption you're holding about why users don't get value from the grip finder, and one thing you'd need to see from customer research to feel confident about moving forward." Activates thinking before the room and means the opportunity mapping exercise doesn't start cold.

Common traps — and how to handle them in the room

Jumping to solutions before the opportunity is agreed on

The facilitator's most important job is pattern recognition here. The moment someone says "we should just..." — redirect explicitly: "That's a great idea, let's park it — but first let's agree which opportunity it would address." Keep the parking lot visible on screen throughout. Honoring the contribution while maintaining structure is more effective than cutting the person off.

HiPPO effect — loudest or most senior voice sets direction

Use the silent individual write before any group discussion for both the opportunity mapping and success definition exercises. Written inputs before spoken inputs levels the playing field and surfaces perspectives that wouldn't survive open debate. When the most senior person goes last in sharing, it reduces anchoring on their view.

Scope creep in the room

Every large project kickoff attracts adjacent ideas — distribution strategy, SEO, personalization, app features. Have a visible parking lot running throughout. It honors every contribution without derailing the agenda. At the close, tell the room explicitly: "These are great — they'll be revisited once research has shaped the core scope."

Treating the OST as a PM artifact rather than a shared canvas

CDH only works if the OST is genuinely a team document. Frame it explicitly: "This tree belongs to everyone in this room. Your job is to challenge it, add to it, and use it to make decisions." If stakeholders don't feel ownership of the tree, they'll disengage from the discovery rhythm within weeks.

Leaving without a documented, confirmed close

The last 5 minutes must be a read-back of what was decided — not what was discussed. The facilitator states it; participants nod or correct. The meeting summary should be a verbatim record of those three decisions, sent within 24 hours. A follow-up document three days later loses the moment of alignment.

Treating research as a formality, not a gate

In the room, be explicit: design work does not begin until the priority opportunity has been validated through customer interviews and assumption tests. The reframe that lands with business stakeholders: "We're not slowing down to do research — we're preventing a redesign that solves the wrong problem."

Interview habit dying after week two

Without a named owner, protected calendar time, and a lightweight log visible to all stakeholders, the weekly interview habit quietly stops when things get busy. Name the owner in the room. Book the first three interview slots before leaving. Make the log accessible to all stakeholders — even a shared doc — so the habit feels like a team activity, not a researcher's private task.

What to walk out of the kickoff with

A signed-off problem statement

One or two sentences agreed on in the room. Example: "The grip finder reaches too few users and converts too few of those who complete it — we need to understand whether the core problem is reach, the quiz experience, or trust in the result before we redesign anything."

A validated and expanded OST — opportunities agreed, solutions blank

The draft tree reshaped by the room. New opportunity nodes added, existing ones pressure-tested, priority branch agreed. The solutions column remains intentionally empty — it fills only as research validates opportunities. This is the living project document going forward, owned by the OST keeper and visible to all stakeholders.

A success definition with 2–3 measurable outcomes

Not a vision statement — specific and measurable. Examples: quiz reach increases from X% to Y% of sessions; quiz-assisted conversion rate improves by Z%; result recommendation confidence score exceeds a threshold in exit surveys. These are the outcomes the team is accountable for, mapped to the OST outcome node.

A prioritized opportunity branch for the first research sprint

Agreed by dot vote and discussion. The branch with the highest impact potential and lowest current evidence is the right starting focus. This shapes the first wave of customer interviews — every question in the interview guide maps back to this branch.

A named discovery owner and committed cadence

A specific person owns the weekly interview habit. A minimum cadence is agreed — weekly if possible, bi-weekly at minimum. The first three interview slots are booked before leaving the room. The log where findings feed the OST is named and accessible to all stakeholders.

A RACI — including the OST keeper role

Who is responsible for research execution, consulted at phase gates, and who makes final calls when the group doesn't reach consensus. The OST keeper is explicitly named — responsible for updating the tree after each interview and experiment, and preparing the monthly stakeholder update.

Two calendar dates booked

First stakeholder OST review (~4 weeks out) and a research cadence check-in (~2 weeks out). Without these set before leaving, the project loses its forcing function within a week. Dates booked in-room have a much higher survival rate than those agreed "to be scheduled."

A parking lot of ideas to revisit post-research

Every good idea that surfaced but was out of scope or premature. Visible during the meeting, distributed in the follow-up summary. Signals to stakeholders their contributions were heard and will be revisited — reduces the urge to re-raise them mid-project and protects the team's focus during research.

A meeting summary sent within 24 hours

Not a minutes document — a decision record. Three sections: what was decided (the three aligned items read back at close), the updated OST, and the parking lot. Sent to all attendees and relevant stakeholders who weren't in the room. The moment of alignment is fragile — document it quickly.

Torres's four assumption types — applied to grip finder before any solution enters design

Every proposed solution rests on a stack of assumptions. Before any solution moves into design, map its assumptions into these four categories. Rank each by importance (how much does the solution depend on this being true?) and evidence (how much do we actually know?). High importance + low evidence = the assumption to test first — with the smallest, cheapest experiment possible.

Desirability — does the customer want this?

Would golfers actually use this if it existed? Do they recognize it as solving a real problem they have?

"Golfers who are uncertain would engage with a 2-question inline module on a product page." / "Users trust a brand recommendation enough to act on it without further validation."

Viability — does this work for the business?

Will the investment generate sufficient return? Does it align with business strategy and constraints?

"Increasing quiz reach will meaningfully lift conversion, not just traffic volume." / "A persistent grip profile will increase return-visit purchase rates enough to justify the engineering cost."

Feasibility — can we actually build this?

Do we have the technical capability, data, and resources to execute this at the quality required?

"The existing quiz logic can be surfaced as an embeddable component on product pages without a full rebuild." / "We can instrument per-question drop-off in GA4 without a significant dev sprint."

Usability — can customers use this as intended?

Will users understand what to do, complete the task, and interpret the output correctly?

"Mobile users can accurately select grip attributes from a visual card interface without needing to zoom." / "Users can measure their own hand size at home using a printed guide without abandoning."

How to run a fast assumption test (CDH)

Torres advocates for the smallest experiment that generates signal — not a full prototype or design sprint. A "fake door" test adds the nav link and measures clicks before the component exists. A one-question exit survey asks "did this recommendation feel right?" A prototype shown to five users in a 20-minute session tests usability for nearly zero cost. The question is always: what is the cheapest way to find out whether this assumption is true or false?

Post-kickoff discovery cadence — CDH-informed continuous loop

Weekly

Customer interview — 30 minutes

One interview per week with a recent grip buyer, prospective buyer, or quiz user. Not a usability test — an opportunity discovery conversation anchored to the priority branch from the kickoff. One team member leads, one observes. Questions follow the interview guide (see next tab). Rotate who leads over time to build the discovery habit across the team, not just the researcher.

Weekly

OST update — add, promote, or retire nodes

Within 24 hours of each interview, the OST keeper updates the tree: new opportunity nodes surfaced, assumptions validated or killed, solutions that now have an evidence base to enter a branch. This takes 15 minutes. The OST is the living record of what the team knows — not a Confluence page updated once a quarter.

Bi-weekly

Assumption test review — 30 minutes

What experiments ran? What did they tell us? Does this change opportunity prioritization? One decision per meeting: does an assumption get promoted (enough evidence), killed (evidence against), or flagged for more testing? This keeps the team moving without waiting for a large research readout before anything can proceed.

Monthly

Stakeholder OST review — 45 minutes

Bring the updated OST to the full stakeholder group. Show what has been learned since last time, which opportunities have shifted in priority based on evidence, and which solutions now have sufficient validation to move into design. This replaces the traditional "big research readout" with a lower-stakes, ongoing alignment rhythm. No single monthly meeting carries too much weight because decisions are made incrementally.

At gate

Opportunity-to-solution promotion decision

A solution earns the right to move into design when two conditions are met: (1) the opportunity it serves has been validated by at least 3 independent customer interviews, and (2) the critical desirability and usability assumptions have been tested with a lightweight experiment. This CDH gate replaces the single large "go / no-go" meeting with a rolling, evidence-based promotion process.

The key shift CDH makes to how this project runs after kickoff

Traditional approach: research phase → readout → design starts. CDH approach: continuous weekly interviews feed the OST, small assumption tests run in parallel, and solutions get promoted into design as they accumulate evidence. The kickoff isn't the start of a project with a waterfall end — it's the start of a loop that doesn't stop. Stakeholders get a monthly view into a living picture instead of a quarterly update on a static roadmap.

Weekly customer interview guide — opportunity discovery

CDH interviews are not usability tests and not surveys. The goal is to understand the customer's actual experience — their context, struggles, and mental model — not to validate solutions. Never show mockups or mention features. Start with a specific recent memory, follow curiosity, stay in the past. Aim for 30 minutes. One person leads, one takes notes.

Warm-up: "Tell me about the last time you bought new grips."

Opens with a specific, recent memory — not a hypothetical. Gets the person talking about real behavior rather than what they think they should say. Listen for: where they were, what triggered the purchase, and whether a recommendation tool would have fit naturally into that moment.

Decision process: "How did you figure out which grips were right for you?"

Maps their actual decision journey. Did they use a tool? Ask a pro? Guess and try? This surfaces the real job-to-be-done — and whether a grip finder would have helped or been ignored entirely. If they mention the Golf Pride quiz, follow it: "Walk me through what that was like." If they didn't mention it, that's significant signal.

Struggle: "Was there anything that made that process harder than it needed to be?"

Direct opportunity probe. Don't suggest answers. If they pause, wait — the silence is often where the most honest answer lives. This is the question that surfaces unmet needs belonging on the OST. A "no, it was fine" is also informative — it may mean the problem lives elsewhere on the tree.

Site experience: "Have you ever visited golfpride.com while shopping for grips? Tell me about that."

Opens the door to their site experience without leading them toward the quiz. If they mention the quiz spontaneously, follow it deeply. If they describe browsing product pages without mentioning the quiz, the reach opportunity is confirmed. If they haven't visited the site at all, that's a distribution and awareness problem upstream of the quiz entirely.

Confidence: "How confident did you feel that you bought the right grip? What gave you that feeling — or didn't?"

Directly probes the trust and result-quality opportunity branch. A buyer who says "I'm still not sure I bought the right one" is describing exactly the problem the quiz is supposed to solve — and hasn't. A buyer who says "the pro shop person explained it" tells you what the quiz needs to replicate in its result copy.

Close: "If you could change one thing about how you found and chose your grips, what would it be?"

Invitation for the customer to name their own solution. Treat these as opportunity signals — not product requirements. Patterns across 5–8 interviews are signal; a single response is anecdote. Track recurring themes and map them back to the OST. If a new theme appears that doesn't fit existing branches, that's a new opportunity node to add.

After the interview — update the OST immediately

Within 24 hours: the OST keeper adds new opportunity nodes, updates confidence levels on existing assumptions, and logs any surprises. A single insight that contradicts a team assumption is worth more than ten that confirm it — contradictions prevent expensive wrong turns in design. After 5–8 interviews on the priority branch, the team should have enough signal to validate or invalidate the core assumptions and make a promotion decision on the first solutions to enter design.
